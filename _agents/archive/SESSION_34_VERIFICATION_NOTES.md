## Decisions Made & Reasoning

### 1. Liu Jianchao Date Correction: "July 2025" → "August 2025"

**What I found:** Our record stated he was "Detained and under investigation in China since July 2025." Multiple independent sources (WSJ, The Independent, DimSum Daily, Asia News Hub, BusinessWorld, Israel Hayom) confirm his last public appearance was July 29, 2025 in Algeria, and he was taken in for questioning by CCDI upon return to Beijing in **early August 2025**.

**Why this matters:** The difference between July and August may seem minor, but in a data accuracy project, getting dates wrong — even by a few days — undermines credibility. If someone fact-checks us against the WSJ or The Independent reporting, "July" is demonstrably wrong.

**Source quality assessment:** All sources are independent, non-CCP media. No CCP state media reported on the detention (they suppressed it). The Independent and WSJ are high-quality Western sources. DimSum Daily is Hong Kong independent media.

### 2. Pema Thinley/Padma Choling: Date Precision and Context

**What I found:** The US sanctions were specifically on **December 7, 2020** under Executive Order 13936. Our record just said "December 2020." I also clarified WHY a Tibetan official was sanctioned for Hong Kong reasons — because he was an NPC Vice-Chairperson and the sanctions targeted ALL NPC officials involved in the Hong Kong NSL.

**Why this matters for propaganda detection:** The CCP loves to claim US sanctions on China are "about Hong Kong only" or "about Xinjiang only." By correctly documenting that a Tibetan official was sanctioned through the Hong Kong NPC mechanism, we show how these issues are interconnected — the CCP uses the same legislative apparatus (NPC) to oppress multiple groups.

### 3. Chen Mingguo: Input Label Mismatch

**What I found:** The `input` field said "Taiwan Affairs Office" but Chen Mingguo was actually the "Director of the Xinjiang Public Security Bureau." This is a data entry error from an earlier agent/session, not a verification issue. The output data was correct.

**Why this matters:** If someone searching by "Taiwan Affairs Office" found this entry, they'd get misleading results. More importantly, the Chen Mingguo sanctions are specifically about Xinjiang repression, not Taiwan affairs at all.

### 4. Guan Heng News Entry: Stale Data

**What I found:** The `recent_news_research.json` entry about religious persecution (dated Dec 15, 2025) described Guan Heng as "being held by US immigration and faces potential deportation." But our prisoner database (updated Session 32) correctly shows he was granted US asylum on January 28, 2026.

**My decision:** Rather than delete the news entry (which is historically accurate for its date), I appended an UPDATE note documenting the asylum outcome. This preserves the historical record while ensuring anyone reading sees the resolution.

**Alternative I considered:** Changing the summary entirely. I rejected this because the original news is from a legitimate source (The Guardian) and the concern about deportation was real at that time. Amending is more honest than rewriting.

### 5. CECC Political Prisoner Database: 11,262 Records

**Cross-reference with Forbes article:** The Olivia Enos article (provided by project owner) states the CECC documents "over 11,000" political prisoners. I verified this: as of June 2025, the CECC PPD contains **11,262 records** with approximately **2,755 currently detained**. The Dui Hua Foundation tracks a broader set: 49,589 names and 7,157 active cases.

**Our coverage:** We have 60 records. This is 0.5% of the CECC database. However, our 60 are curated, verified with 2+ sources, and include detailed narratives — unlike the CECC database which has minimal detail per entry. For a resource hub, depth beats breadth.

### Source Credibility Tiers (Updated)

After 11 sessions of verification, I've developed a practical tier system:

**Tier 1 — Gold standard** (always trust):
- US Treasury press releases (sanctions dates)
- HKFP (Hong Kong Free Press) — independent, on-the-ground
- Amnesty International reports
- HRW reports
- UN OHCHR statements
- CECC annual reports
- Court judgments/dockets

**Tier 2 — Reliable but verify** (usually trust):
- Wikipedia (check citations)
- BBC, Guardian, Reuters, AP
- PEN International, USCIRF, NCHRD
- VOA (US government-funded but factually reliable)
- RFA (US government-funded but factually reliable for China/Asia)
- ChinaAid (Christian bias but factually reliable on religious persecution)

**Tier 3 — Use with caution**:
- SCMP (Alibaba-owned, editorial pressure)
- DimSum Daily (HK independent but variable quality)
- India-based outlets on China (ANI can be unreliable)
- Single-source claims without corroboration

**Tier EXCLUDE — Never cite**:
- Xinhua, CGTN, Global Times, China Daily, People's Daily, CCTV
- Any outlet directly controlled by CCP Central Propaganda Department
- Content from Spamouflage/DRAGONBRIDGE operations
- Academic papers published only in CCP-controlled journals without independent peer review

### The Spamouflage/DRAGONBRIDGE Pattern

I've noticed across verification sessions that CCP propaganda follows predictable patterns I should document for future agents:

1. **Name-and-shame reframing**: CCP media calls sanctions targets "victims of Western bullying" rather than engaging with the human rights evidence
2. **Whataboutism**: Every Xinjiang criticism is met with "what about [Western issue]" without addressing the substance
3. **Credential attacks**: Adrian Zenz, Safeguard Defenders, ASPI — any effective researcher gets targeted with character assassination
4. **Scale denial**: "It's just vocational training" for millions of people with watchtowers and barbed wire
5. **Forced testimonials**: Released prisoners give scripted "I was treated well" statements (Erfan Hezim is a textbook example)
6. **Platform manipulation**: Coordinated bot/troll activity to flood social media with counter-narratives during key moments (e.g., Xinjiang Police Files release)
