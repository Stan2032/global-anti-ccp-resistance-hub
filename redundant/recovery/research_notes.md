# Research Notes: CCP Propaganda on Twitter/X

## Background and Context

### Evolution of CCP Propaganda

- CCP propaganda has evolved from primarily domestic focus to international influence campaigns
- Initially focused on promoting positive narratives about China and countering criticism
- After observing Russia's success in 2016 US election interference, China adopted similar tactics
- Major shift during COVID-19 pandemic when Chinese officials began pushing conspiracy theories about virus origins
- Chinese foreign ministry spokesperson Zhao Lijian's March 2020 tweet suggesting US military brought the virus to Wuhan marked a turning point
- CCP propaganda has increasingly adopted Russian-style disinformation techniques

### Key Organizations and Infrastructure

- Central Propaganda Department of the CCP oversees propaganda efforts
- Ministry of Public Security (MPS) runs the "912 Special Project Working Group" for online disinformation
- State media outlets (CGTN, China Daily, Global Times, Xinhua) have official presence on Twitter/X
- Despite Twitter/X being blocked in mainland China, the government maintains extensive operations on the platform

## Scale and Presence on Twitter/X

### Official Presence

- Chinese diplomats and government officials maintain active Twitter/X accounts
- State media outlets have official accounts with large followings
- "Wolf warrior diplomacy" involves officials using confrontational rhetoric on Twitter/X

### Covert Networks

- "Spamouflage" or "Dragonbridge" network consists of hundreds of thousands of fake accounts
- Meta removed nearly 8,000 accounts in Q2 2023 alone
- Google/YouTube shut down over 100,000 associated accounts in recent years
- X/Twitter has blocked hundreds of thousands of China "state-backed" accounts
- Many accounts remain active on X/Twitter even after being identified and removed from other platforms
- According to Washington Post analysis, 136 out of 150 accounts identified by Meta were still active on X as of February 2024
- Of 123 accounts identified as part of China-based campaigns, all but 8 remained on X

### Scale of Operations

- Meta reported that China-based campaigns have been multiplying
- Of 10 networks taken down by Meta since 2017, 6 were identified in the past year
- The Chinese government has built what CNN describes as "the world's largest known online disinformation operation"
- US Department of Justice complaint named 34 individual officers with China's Ministry of Public Security involved in disinformation campaigns
- The operation appears to involve "hundreds" of MPS officers across China

## Propaganda Techniques and Tactics

### Account Creation and Management

- Use of AI-generated profile pictures to create fake personas
- Hijacking dormant accounts that previously posted in other languages
- Creating accounts with randomly generated usernames
- Coordinated posting schedules that align with Beijing working hours
- Posts show "bursts of activity in the mid-morning and early afternoon, Beijing time, with breaks for lunch and supper"

### Content Strategies

- "Flooding" hashtags with junk content to dilute legitimate messages
- "Astroturfing" - creating appearance of grassroots support through coordinated actions
- Some accounts create original content while others amplify through likes, shares, and comments
- Use of inflammatory cartoons to ridicule critics of China
- Targeting specific individuals with harassment campaigns (e.g., journalist Jiayang Fan faced 12,000+ tweets with the same hashtag)
- Impersonating critics to confuse audiences and "muddy the waters"

### Narrative Themes

- Delegitimizing the West, particularly the US
- Highlighting US social problems (racism, gun violence, etc.)
- Denying human rights abuses in Xinjiang
- Promoting China as a friend to the world rather than an authoritarian state
- Stoking controversy around divisive US issues (gun laws, race politics)
- Spreading COVID-19 origin conspiracy theories
- Attacking critics of China with personal smears

### Evolution of Tactics

- Initially focused on issues domestically relevant to China
- Now increasingly stoking controversy around global issues
- Adopting more sophisticated techniques over time
- Using multiple platforms simultaneously (Twitter/X, Facebook, YouTube, even Pinterest)
- Increasingly targeting downballot elections in the US

## Identifying CCP Propaganda

### Account Characteristics

- AI-generated profile pictures (identifiable by consistent eye placement, blurred edges around hair, teeth at strange angles)
- Recently created accounts with few followers
- Usernames that appear randomly generated
- High levels of activity pushing propaganda narratives
- Repeated use of the same hashtags
- Accounts that previously posted in other languages suddenly posting Chinese content
- Poor English with grammatical errors suggesting machine translation

### Content Patterns

- Identical posts across multiple accounts
- Coordinated posting times
- Excessive use of the same hashtags
- Cartoons depicting critics of China in derogatory ways
- Narratives that closely align with official CCP positions
- Focus on divisive US issues rather than substantive policy discussions

## Influence and Effectiveness

### Impact Assessment

- Most accounts receive little engagement, rarely going viral
- Primary goal appears to be muddying discourse rather than changing minds
- Effective at drowning out legitimate conversations through sheer volume
- Creates atmosphere of confusion and uncertainty around facts
- Particularly targets critics of China with harassment to silence them
- Creates sense of fear among targets (e.g., "Every day I live in a sense of fear" - Jiajun Qiu)

### Platform Responses

- Meta, YouTube, and other platforms have taken down thousands of accounts
- Twitter/X has been largely absent from industry coordination efforts since Elon Musk's acquisition
- X no longer sends representatives to biweekly meetings where companies share notes on fake account networks
- This allows propaganda to spread from X back to other platforms

## Common Narratives and Debunks

### Narrative: COVID-19 originated in the US

**Claim:** The virus was brought to Wuhan by the US military.

**Debunk:** Scientific consensus points to natural origins, with the virus likely jumping from animals to humans. Multiple independent studies have traced the earliest cases to the Huanan Seafood Market in Wuhan. No credible evidence supports the claim of US military involvement.

### Narrative: There is no oppression of Uyghurs in Xinjiang

**Claim:** Reports of detention camps are "lies fabricated by the United States and the West."

**Debunk:** Extensive documentation from multiple independent sources, including satellite imagery, witness testimonies, leaked government documents, and investigations by human rights organizations confirm the existence of detention facilities. The UN High Commissioner for Human Rights released a report in 2022 concluding that China's actions in Xinjiang may constitute crimes against humanity.

### Narrative: The US has a poor human rights record and no standing to criticize others

**Claim:** US issues like racism and gun violence make it hypocritical for America to criticize human rights in China.

**Debunk:** While the US does face domestic human rights challenges, this "whataboutism" fallacy attempts to deflect legitimate criticism. Human rights are universal standards that apply to all countries. The existence of problems in one country doesn't negate the responsibility to address human rights violations elsewhere.

### Narrative: Western democracy is failing while China's system is superior

**Claim:** China's governance model delivers stability and economic growth while Western democracies are chaotic and declining.

**Debunk:** While China has achieved economic growth, it has come with significant costs to civil liberties, environmental protection, and long-term sustainability. Democratic systems, despite their flaws, provide mechanisms for peaceful power transitions, checks on authority, and protection of individual rights that are absent in authoritarian systems.

## Conclusion

CCP propaganda on Twitter/X represents a significant and growing effort to shape global narratives and influence public opinion. The scale of these operations has expanded dramatically in recent years, with increasingly sophisticated tactics being deployed. While the effectiveness of these campaigns in changing minds may be limited, they succeed in creating confusion, amplifying divisions, and intimidating critics. The reduced moderation on Twitter/X since Elon Musk's acquisition has made the platform particularly vulnerable to these influence operations.
